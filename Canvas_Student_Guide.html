MS Project Survival Guide: HQ Relocation

Welcome to the Project Manager's Toolkit! If you are feeling stuck while building out your MS Project schedule, this guide is designed to nudge you in the right direction without giving away the answers. Use these hints to troubleshoot your timeline, calendar, and resources.

Phase 1: Project Calendar & Constraints

The Issue: You need to ensure the IT Networking Team does not work between November 10th and 14th, but this isn't a company-wide holiday.

Hint: You have two options here. You can either create a specific "Base Calendar" just for the IT Networking Team and assign it to them in the Resource Sheet, OR you can enter it as an Exception in the standard project calendar if you want to ensure absolutely no work happens on the project during those days. Think about which method reflects reality best based on the project requirements!

The Issue: You entered Thanksgiving and Winter Break into the calendar exceptions, but tasks are still scheduling over them.

Hint: Adding exceptions to the "Standard" calendar is only step one. Check your Project Information settings. Is your project actually using the calendar you just modified? Also, double-check that you entered the correct start and finish dates for the exception (especially multi-day breaks!).

Phase 2: Task Dependencies & Sequencing

The Issue: You need to account for 15 days of waiting time for furniture, but nobody is actually "working" those 15 days, so assigning a resource to it feels wrong.

Hint: In Project Management, waiting time between two linked tasks is called Lag Time. Look at the predecessor column. Instead of just entering the task ID number (like "5"), try exploring how to add lag (e.g., "5FS+15 days"). Alternatively, you can create a 15-day task called "Wait for Furniture Delivery" and simply leave the resource assigned blank so it costs the project $0.

The Issue: Your Gantt chart looks like a giant vertical wall instead of a waterfall.

Hint: This means you haven't linked your tasks yet! Read through the emails in the intake portal carefully. Look for trigger words like "until", "once", and "after". Use the Predecessors column to link the task ID numbers logically. For example, if Task 4 cannot start until Task 3 finishes, the predecessor for Task 4 is 3.

Phase 3: Resource Allocation & Costs

The Issue: When you assign the "New Office Furniture" to a task, MS Project is trying to charge you $15,000 per hour instead of a flat fee.

Hint: Open your Resource Sheet. Look at the "Type" column. By default, MS Project assumes every resource is "Work" (meaning a human being paid hourly). Desks and Servers are physical items with a fixed cost. Try changing their Type to "Material" and entering the price in the "Cost/Use" or "Std. Rate" column appropriately.

The Issue: A little red person icon appeared in your indicator column, meaning a resource is working more hours than they physically have in a day.

Hint: Look at your Gantt Chart on the right side. Do you have two tasks happening at the exact same time (overlapping) with the exact same resource assigned to both? To fix this, you either need to change the dependencies so the tasks happen one after the other, assign a different resource to one of the tasks, or check if the "Max Units" in your Resource Sheet accurately reflects how many people are on that team.

Final Sanity Check

The Auto-Schedule vs. Manually Schedule Trap

Check the "Task Mode" column on the far left of your task list. Are there little thumbtack icons? If so, your tasks are Manually Scheduled, which means MS Project will ignore your predecessors and calendars! Highlight your tasks, go to the Task tab, and click Auto Schedule to let the software do the heavy lifting.
